Healthcare · iOS + Android

Designing trust for doctors in a home healthcare marketplace

How I helped Dr. Morepen onboard and retain qualified doctors by reducing uncertainty, improving operational confidence, and creating a workflow that made home consultations feel safe, reliable, and worthwhile.

My Role Senior Product Designer
Platform iOS + Android
Type 0→1 Product Design

The Challenge

Getting doctors to say yes

Dr. Morepen saw an opportunity to bring healthcare directly to patients' homes. But unlike food delivery or ride-sharing services, healthcare depends on a trusted supply network of qualified professionals.
To scale the service successfully, we first needed to earn doctors' trust.

Awareness

Doctor receives a home visit request.

Evaluation

Doctor assesses travel time, patient details, and feasibility.

Adoption

Platform provides transparency, flexibility, and safety assurances.

Visit Completed

Doctor accepts requests consistently and becomes an active provider.

Testing the Concept

Validating the concept with a lean MVP

Rather than investing months into building a feature-rich platform, we started with a focused MVP designed to answer one critical question:

Would doctors adopt a structured home-visit workflow?

Understanding how quickly doctors accept home visit requests

We wanted to understand how quickly doctors respond to incoming home visit requests and whether urgency could improve acceptance rates.

  • Added a countdown to encourage faster decisions
  • Tracked how many requests were accepted vs ignored
  • Used this to understand real doctor response behaviour

Helping doctors feel safe during visits

Even after accepting a request, uncertainty remained.
To improve transparency and accountability, we introduced:

  • Address verification checks
  • Live arrival confirmation
  • Time stamped location records

Reducing manual effort during consultations

  • Automated recording so doctors don’t have to remember to manually document while treating patients
  • Used step-by-step prompts to guide doctors starting with prescription, followed by treatment details

Validation that earned a Phase 2

Phase 2

Reducing cancellations with flexible arrival options

Problems

  • Heavy traffic in Gurugram frequently caused delays, creating friction for doctors.
  • Patients cancelled instead of waiting without clarity/li>

Solutions

  • Introduced 30 and 45 minute arrival windows for flexibility
  • Allowed doctors to adjust timings without cancelling
  • Notified patients in advance with updated arrival times

Helping doctors recommend next steps

During consultations, doctors often identify the need for additional care like tests, medicines, or follow-ups.

  • Suggested relevant follow-up options
  • Doctors review and select what’s appropriate
  • Shown to patients as a clear care plan
Final impact

Outcomes that compounded

Once we made the system more flexible, things started working better for everyone. Doctors were more comfortable accepting requests, and more patients ended up getting the care they needed.

Key Learnings

What I learned

Flexibility Drives Reliability

Phase 1 taught me that the "Happy Path" (Doctor arrives in 30 mins) is a myth. The real design work happens in the edge cases.

Trust Through Transparency

In on-demand healthcare, trust isn't an emotion; it’s a system state. By using GPS-Selfie, I learned that you can engineer security into a platform. If you remove the "human choice" from high-risk moments (like starting a recording), you remove the "human error" that breaks trust.

Healthcare · iOS + Android

Gamifying healthcare to build lasting health habits

How game mechanics, virtual companions, and thoughtful UX writing made medication adherence feel rewarding instead of clinical.

My Role Product Designer
Platform iOS + Android
Published Behance
Health Habits App
The problem

Medication non-adherence is a design problem

Over 50% of patients with chronic conditions don't take their medication as prescribed. The reasons are human — forgetting, fatigue, feeling better, or simply losing motivation. Existing apps addressed the symptom (reminders) but not the cause (sustained engagement).

"The problem wasn't that people didn't know they needed to take their medication. It was that nothing made them want to."

Solution approach

A companion, not a clipboard

Instead of another clinical reminder tool, we designed a health companion experience centred on a virtual pet. Taking your medication helps your companion grow. Skipping it has visible consequences. The emotional stakes made adherence personal.

  • Medication reminders tied to companion health — creating emotional accountability
  • Virtual consultations for habit coaching, reducing dependency on push notifications alone
  • Vital sign tracking gamified through daily streaks and companion upgrades
  • Progress visible not just in numbers, but in the companion's visual state
Companion System Design
Key design considerations

Designing for consistency, not novelty

Game mechanics can backfire if they feel manipulative. The design principle throughout was: reward the effort, not the streak. Missing a day shouldn't feel punishing — it should feel motivating to return.

Reflection

What made this hard

Healthcare design carries real ethical weight. We constantly balanced engagement design against the risk of making users feel guilty for being human. The companion mechanic worked precisely because it expressed care, not pressure.
